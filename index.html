The FFS Contribution Margin Case

            
Foundation — Understanding the base model before adding complexity

          

        


        

          
📋 Context

          
You are the CFO of a 450-bed regional health system. Your ambulatory division schedules 40,000 outpatient visits/year with a 20% overall no-show rate. Staff coding revealed transportation barriers account for 30% of missed appointments—consistent with national LMI population estimates.

          
A county transit authority proposes co-sponsoring an NEMT pilot at $60/round trip (10% overhead), with an estimated 50% mitigation rate. A community bank has committed $50,000 in CRA-eligible funding. Net revenue per kept visit: $225. Marginal clinical cost: $90. LMI share: 90%. All trips are within the CRA Assessment Area.

          

            
📚 Evidence Anchor

            "Transportation barriers are a well-documented driver of missed care, delayed care, and poorer chronic disease management—making transportation a direct lever for both revenue recovery and quality performance."
            CMS VBID Transportation Use Case (CY2023) + NEMT systematic meta-analysis (PMC9026972)
          

        


        

          
🔮 Prediction Challenge

          
Before calculating — predict the expected first-year net benefit from this NEMT pilot:

          

            
A. ~$40,000–$60,000 — modest positive, breakeven territory

            
B. ~$80,000–$90,000 — meaningful positive, ROI slightly above 1×

            
C. ~$150,000–$200,000 — strong positive, ROI above 2×

            
D. Negative — trip costs exceed recovered margin

          

          

            
✓ Correct answer: B — approximately $82,800 net benefit, ROI ≈ 1.05×

            

              Transport no-shows = 40,000 × 0.20 × 0.30 = 2,400

              Prevented = 2,400 × 0.50 = 1,200 visits

              Gross revenue = 1,200 × $225 = $270,000

              Marginal cost = 1,200 × $90 = $108,000

              Transport cost = 1,200 × $60 = $72,000 + overhead $7,200 = $79,200

              Net benefit = $270,000 − $108,000 − $79,200 = $82,800
            

          

          
        


        

          
🪞 Learner Reflection

          
    
            
  • What assumption drives the most uncertainty — mitigation rate, trip cost, or no-show rate? Why?
    • 
            
  • At what trip cost does this program break even? How would you use that figure in vendor negotiations?
    • 
            
  • A 1.05× ROI may disappoint a board used to higher returns. How do you frame this as a platform investment, not just a financial return?
    • 
            
  • Why use marginal clinical cost rather than fully-loaded cost? What would happen to the ROI if you switched?
    • 
          

          
        


        

          
🏛️ Expert Analysis

          

            
The FFS contribution margin ROI is conservative by design. The $82,800 net benefit represents only direct financial recovery from prevented no-shows—it excludes chronic disease management effects, care management billing, and quality performance value. This is the floor, not the ceiling.

            
Break-even: Maximum viable trip cost = net revenue − marginal cost = $225 − $90 = $135/trip. Your $60 trip provides $75 of headroom—a powerful vendor negotiating position and a clear "scale ceiling" for program expansion.

            
Board framing: A 1.05× FFS ROI growing to 1.2–1.4× by Year 3 (efficiency gains + volume) is a compelling platform story when CCM/TCM and VBC layers are added. Present it as a foundation investment. The FFS layer alone is positive; the full investment case is substantially stronger.

            
Marginal vs. fully-loaded cost: Fully-loaded cost ($180–$220/visit for a typical FQHC) would make the ROI negative—but this would be economically misleading. Existing clinical capacity is not newly purchased for recovered visits. The marginal cost model reflects actual incremental financial impact, which is the correct decision-making lens.

            

              Nuance: The Chaiyachati (2018) pragmatic rideshare trial found no significant reduction in no-shows due to low uptake. Program design, patient engagement, and eligibility targeting matter as much as the financial model.
            

          

        


        

          
        

      


      
      

        

          02
          

            
The Coding & Documentation Layer

            
Z-codes, CPT capture, and incremental documentation value

          

        


        

          
📋 Context

          
Building on Exercise 1: your 1,200 recovered visits create an opportunity. Your informatics team proposes systematic SDOH Z-code screening (Z75.3 — transportation barrier) for NEMT-enabled patients at point of care. Estimated 25% Z-code capture rate among recovered visits (conservative), with $20 average reimbursement uplift per Z-coded encounter. Separately, a CPT-level coding improvement reaches 15% of recovered visits at $15 uplift each.

          
Your coding director confirms: ICD-10-CM Z-codes for social determinants have been reportable as additional diagnoses since 2016. Some payers now incorporate SDOH documentation into quality incentive programs.

        


        

          
🔮 Prediction Challenge

          
Predict the combined coding uplift from these two documentation improvements:

          

            
A. Under $5,000 — too conservative to matter

            
B. $6,000–$10,000 — meaningful but small relative to FFS base

            
C. $12,000–$15,000 — noteworthy secondary revenue

            
D. Over $20,000 — significant relative to program cost

          

          

            
✓ Correct: B — approximately $8,700 combined uplift

            

              Z-code uplift = 1,200 × 25% × $20 = $6,000

              CPT uplift = 1,200 × 15% × $15 = $2,700

              Total coding uplift = $8,700

              Combined net (FFS + coding) = $82,800 + $8,700 = $91,500 | ROI = 1.15×
            

          

          
        


        

          
🪞 Learner Reflection

          
    
            
  • $8,700 is ~11% of your FFS net benefit. Is this worth the workflow investment for consistent Z-code documentation?
    • 
            
  • What governance and QA processes ensure Z-code documentation is accurate and audit-ready?
    • 
            
  • Your compliance team flags that CPT content is AMA-licensed IP. How does this affect your training platform and documentation tools?
    • 
            
  • Z-code documentation has value beyond reimbursement. Name two non-financial benefits of capturing SDOH barriers systematically in your EHR.
    • 
          

          
        


        

          
🏛️ Expert Analysis

          

            
The coding layer is additive but requires discipline. $8,700 in this scenario is modest in dollars, but the organizational habit—screening for SDOH barriers, documenting consistently, linking to care response—has compounding value beyond reimbursement.

            
Non-financial benefits: (1) Z-code documentation creates a population health registry of patients with transportation barriers, enabling proactive outreach. (2) Systematic SDOH documentation supports CHNA evidence requirements (IRS Schedule H) and CRA defensibility—both central to this platform's regulatory use case.

            
CPT licensing: AMA holds CPT copyright. Reference CMS MLN documents (which reproduce allowable CPT content under federal government license) rather than raw CPT descriptors when building training platforms or documentation tools.

            
High-performing programs: A 25% Z-code capture rate is deliberately conservative. Programs with EHR workflow integration and staff training report 60–80% capture rates. At 60%, Z-code uplift alone reaches $14,400—and the SDOH registry value becomes proportionally larger.

📋 Context

          
CCM: Of your 40,000 annual visits, 500 patients have 2+ chronic conditions qualifying for CCM. Current enrollment rate: 40%. The NEMT-CCM strategy is operational — the reliable contact created by rides enables genuine clinical staff time (med reconciliation, care plan review, care coordination). The ride creates the window; the clinical work fills it.

            
TCM aligns naturally: The 7/14-day face-to-face window is exactly where transportation barriers cause harm. A patient discharged Friday afternoon without transportation misses the 7-day window. The NEMT contribution is the incremental patients enrolled and months billed because the contact window enabled consistent threshold completion." The VBC Quality Threshold Layer

            
When kept appointments become measurable quality dollars

          

        


        

          
📋 Context

          
Your health system participates in ACO REACH. CMS has structured a quality withhold of $500,000. Your baseline quality score: 72/100. Quality improvement projects that if NEMT-enabled appointment completion improves adherence to four key measures, your composite could reach 79/100.

          
The four measures affected by missed appointments: Diabetes Glycemic Status >9% (QPP Measure 001), Controlling High Blood Pressure (Measure 236), Colorectal Cancer Screening (Measure 113), and Transitions of Care/Medication Reconciliation (Star Ratings). Each requires a completed clinical encounter within a measurement window.

          
Contract uses a linear earn-back model: quality score maps linearly to withhold recovery across the 100-point scale.

          

            
📚 VBC Policy Anchors

            "In ACO REACH, CMS applies a quality withhold earned back based on a total quality score."
            CMS ACO REACH PY 2026 Model Update Quick Reference + QPP Measure Specifications
          

        


        

          
🔮 Prediction Challenge

          
What is the incremental quality earn-back from a 7-point quality score improvement on a $500,000 withhold pool?

          

            
A. $7,000 — 1% of pool per point

            
B. $35,000 — proportional linear recovery

            
C. $50,000 — threshold step-up bonus

            
D. Cannot be calculated without the contract floor and ceiling

          

          

            
✓ Correct: B — $35,000 (linear); D is also defensible

            

              Linear earn-back: Incremental = (79 − 72) / 100 × $500,000 = 7% × $500,000 = $35,000


              D is valid: real contracts have floors, ceilings, and non-linear steps. ACO REACH earn-back methodology is more complex; this exercise uses a simplified linear model for pedagogical clarity.
            

          

          
        


        

          
🪞 Learner Reflection

          
    
            
  • The "quality-threshold bridge" says missed visits reduce both (1) opportunities to deliver care and (2) opportunities to document it measure-countably. Why does documentation matter independently in a VBC model?
    • 
            
  • If your contract uses a cliff model ($150K bonus only if you cross the 75-point threshold), how does this change the analysis for a 3-point vs. 7-point improvement?
    • 
            
  • You can't definitively attribute the 7-point improvement entirely to NEMT. Design a study to isolate NEMT's contribution to quality performance.
    • 
            
  • Name one measure where NEMT impact is direct and high-confidence, and one where attribution is uncertain.
    • 
          

          
        


        

          
🏛️ Expert Analysis

          

            
The quality layer is the most strategically significant but hardest to attribute. $35,000 in earn-back is compelling, but requires two defensible claims: (1) quality score improved, and (2) NEMT contributed meaningfully.

            
High-confidence attribution — Transitions of Care: NEMT → face-to-face visit → medication reconciliation documented → Star measure potentially closed. This is the highest-confidence chain. A 55% → 75% TCM completion rate directly traces to a measurable quality outcome.

            
Lower-confidence — Diabetes glycemic status: A kept appointment enables an HbA1c draw, which is necessary but not sufficient. The draw must be ordered, performed, and below threshold in the measurement period. NEMT improves probability at each step but doesn't guarantee closure.

            
Cliff vs. linear: In a cliff model, 72 → 74.9 earns $0; 74.9 → 75.1 earns $150,000. This creates extremely high marginal ROI near thresholds—making NEMT a strategically critical investment in the right contract year. Always model both structures for leadership.
